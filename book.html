Chapter One
Regulatory Foundations of Consumer Credit Protection

Credit in the United States is not simply a marketplace of lenders and borrowers. It is an ecosystem regulated by several powerful institutions whose job is to maintain order, fairness, and transparency. These institutions protect consumers from unfair reporting, deceptive practices, and systemic abuse, while also ensuring that the three major credit bureaus — TransUnion, Equifax, and Experian — comply with federal and state law.

This chapter examines the origins, missions, and modern functions of the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), and the Office of the Attorney General (with a focus on Texas). It also explains how each regulator influences credit reporting practices and serves as an enforcement mechanism that ultimately shapes how the credit bureaus behave.

1. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

1.1 Historical Foundation

The FTC was established under the Federal Trade Commission Act of 1914, 15 U.S.C. §§ 41–58, originally focused on antitrust enforcement. Over time, its mission expanded to cover consumer protection, including credit reporting and debt collection.
